A few years ago, if someone had told me I’d graduate from Georgia Tech with a Master’s degree in Computer Science specializing in Artificial Intelligence, I probably wouldn’t have believed them.

My background was in mechanical engineering.

I worked on real-world engineering systems, vehicle structures, manufacturing problems, and product development. Artificial Intelligence felt like something distant, a field dominated by researchers, mathematicians, and people who had been coding since childhood.

But over time, something became obvious:

AI was no longer optional.

It was becoming the operating system for modern engineering, software, automation, research, and even creativity itself.

So I made a decision that completely changed the direction of my career.

I enrolled in Georgia Tech’s OMSCS program with a specialization in Artificial Intelligence while working full time, balancing life responsibilities, raising a family, and trying to survive semesters that regularly pushed me beyond my limits.

And somewhere along the journey, something interesting happened.

I stopped seeing AI as magic.

I started seeing it as systems.

#### The Problem With Most AI Content Online

Most people today use AI tools every day:

- ChatGPT
- Claude
- Gemini
- Cursor
- Copilot
- Midjourney
- Perplexity

But very few people actually understand:

- why these systems work,
- how transformers changed everything,
- what embeddings really are,
- why RAG exists,
- how AI agents operate,
- or where the entire industry is heading.

Most tutorials online fall into one of two extremes:

#### Extreme 1 - Too Academic

You open a tutorial and immediately see:

- matrix multiplication,
- probability theory,
- transformer equations,
- attention heatmaps,
- and research jargon.

Beginners get overwhelmed instantly.

#### Extreme 2 - Too Superficial

Other tutorials skip the foundations entirely and jump straight into:

- “Build an AI app in 10 minutes”
- “Use this framework”
- “Copy this API code”

The problem?

People learn how to call APIs without understanding the systems underneath.

That creates users.

Not engineers.

#### What This Series Will Be Different

This series is designed to bridge that gap.

The goal is not just to teach AI terminology.

The goal is to help you deeply understand modern AI systems step by step until you can eventually build real-world AI applications confidently.

We are going to start from the absolute fundamentals and progressively evolve toward advanced AI engineering topics.

Not through hype.

Not through shortcuts.

But through intuition, systems thinking, visuals, architecture breakdowns, and practical projects.

#### The Biggest Mistake Beginners Make

Most people try learning advanced AI concepts in the wrong order.

For example:

- learning RAG before embeddings,
- learning agents before tool calling,
- learning transformers before tokenization,
- learning fine-tuning before understanding inference.

That creates memorization instead of understanding.

This series is intentionally structured differently.

Each topic builds on the previous one.

The goal is to make difficult concepts feel natural instead of intimidating.

<p class="section-break">. . .</p>

#### Why I’m Writing This Publicly

Honestly, this is also the series I wish existed when I started learning AI.

When I first entered the field, I constantly felt like everyone else already understood concepts that seemed impossible to me.

Transformers felt confusing.  
Embeddings felt abstract.  
Agents sounded like science fiction.

But once I started breaking these systems down visually and structurally, the confusion disappeared.

I realized modern AI is not magic.

It is engineering.

Complex engineering, yes.

But still understandable.

And that realization changed everything for me.

So instead of keeping these notes, diagrams, experiments, and lessons private, I decided to publish the entire journey publicly.

One article at a time.

One concept at a time.

One system at a time.

#### The Future Belongs to Builders

AI is moving extremely fast.

But here’s the interesting part:

Most people are still consuming AI.

Very few people are learning how to engineer AI systems.

That gap is going to matter.

A lot.

The next generation of engineers will not just use AI tools.

They will:

- design workflows,
- orchestrate agents,
- connect systems,
- optimize inference,
- build retrieval pipelines,

That’s the direction this series is heading.
